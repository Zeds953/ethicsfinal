Ethical Considerations in Data Governance

A Crisis of Data Trust & Autonomy

In today’s digital environment, data collection is engrained into nearly every aspect of life—from browsing the web and using apps to interacting with smart devices and social media. Companies collect enormous amounts of personal data to drive targeted advertising, develop predictive algorithms, and build innovative services. This data is often collected passively or under unclear terms, with little understanding or consent from users.

At the heart of this is a powerful economic model: data equals profit. The more personal information a company can collect and analyze, the more accurately it can predict behavior, influence decisions, and monetize user activity. While these data-driven technologies have led to innovation,like personalized healthcare, smarter cities, and AI-powered tools, they also raise serious ethical concerns.

The ethical dilemma is clear: How can society balance the benefits of data-driven technologies with the fundamental human right to privacy, autonomy, and informed consent, especially when current practices often obscure how personal information is exploited?

This dilemma is intensified by a lack of transparency, weak regulation, and a growing power imbalance between individuals and the corporations that control their data. Users are often unaware of the extent to which their data is collected or how it is used, creating a system where corporations hold immense power over the personal lives of millions. The result is a widespread erosion of privacy, a loss of control over personal information, and limited accountability for those who profit from it.

Understanding Why Things Are As They Are

The current data governance crisis didn’t emerge overnight,it’s the result of several contributing factors:

Tech Outpacing Regulation: Innovation has moved faster than lawmakers can respond. As new data-driven technologies emerge, outdated or nonexistent regulations leave gaps in user protection.

Surveillance Capitalism: Many business models now rely on monetizing personal data. Companies profit from tracking user behavior, targeting ads, and selling insights which creates strong financial incentives to maintain their questionable practices.

Public Unawareness and Resignation: For years, users lacked awareness about the extent of data collection. Even as awareness grows, many feel powerless to opt out, leading to a feeling of apathy rather than resistance.

Complex Data Ecosystems: Data flows through vast, interconnected systems that are shared, sold, and analyzed by multiple parties. This complexity makes it hard for individuals to fully understand where their data goes or how it’s used.

Weak or Delayed Legislation: In countries like the U.S., lobbying from powerful tech firms has slowed and diluted privacy laws. Without strong a strong federal-level freame work, like the EU’s GDPR, there’s little accountability or oversight.

Impact and Dimensions

Understanding the data governance crisis requires looking at its broad societal, technological, economic, and political impacts. Each dimension reveals a unique aspect of why the issue is so difficult to resolve and so harmful when left unaddressed.

Social Dimensions

The unchecked collection and use of personal data affects individual behavior and societal norms. People may self-censor online due to perceived surveillance, a phenomenon known as the “chilling effect.” Algorithmic bias in areas like hiring and lending can result in systemic discrimination. Trust in digital platforms continues to erode, particularly among vulnerable populations disproportionately affected by current data practices. .

Technological Dimensions

Surveillance tools like facial recognition, IoT devices, and behavioral tracking are now embedded in daily life. Many operate using “black box” algorithms that lack transparency or accountability. Data breaches have become increasingly common, exposing sensitive personal data. Even anonymized data can often be re-identified with advanced tools, posing ongoing privacy risks. These same tools can also be misused in AI systems with little oversight.

Economic Dimensions

Personal data has become one of the most valuable assets in the digital economy. Data brokers and ad-tech firms profit from compiling and selling detailed consumer profiles. A handful of large tech companies dominate the market, reducing competition. The financial incentives to collect more data often outweigh any motivation to implement strong privacy protections. Meanwhile, data breaches and cybercrime cost businesses and individuals billions of dollars annually.

Political Dimensions

Personal data has been weaponized in political campaigns through micro-targeted ads and disinformation. Some governments use surveillance powers with little oversight, threatening civil liberties. Tech companies frequently lobby against privacy regulations, stalling meaningful reform. Internationally, data flows across borders without consistent governance, complicating enforcement and raising concerns about sovereignty and rights.

Expert Perspective: A Cybersecurity Instructor’s View

Damon Sharretts, a cybersecurity instructor at Northwood Technical College in New Richmond, Wisconsin, emphasized the unique role cybersecurity professionals play in understanding the risks of unchecked data use:


“They’re gonna have a better understanding of how the data can be used from a negative perspective. What companies are able to access, uncover, or use the data for that negatively affects most ‘normal’ users.”


He also highlighted the risks posed by emerging technologies, particularly AI in sensitive areas like healthcare:


“It’s probably what the AI is capable of doing with the data… Being able to dig deeper into that data with AI analysis and make automated decisions… such as for approvals or denials, that would be a big issue.”


These insights underscore both the ethical and technical urgency of reforming data practices before harm becomes ingrained in the system.

Key Hurdles to Resolution

  • Technological Complexity & Pace of Change: The rapid development of new technologies, like AI, consistently outpaces regulatory efforts, leaving laws outdated before they’re even implemented.
  • Powerful Economic Incentives: Many business models, especially those of major tech companies, depend on the collection of personal data, creating strong resistance to regulations that might limit data access.
  • Global Nature of the Internet & Data Flows: Data flows freely across borders, making it difficult for any one country to enforce its laws, with conflicts such as GDPR versus U.S. data transfer policies highlighting this issue.
  • Lack of Public Awareness & Digital Literacy: Many people don’t fully understand how their data is used, which leads to a lack of public pressure for stronger protections.
  • Political Power & Corporate Lobbying: Well-funded tech industry lobbying can delay or weaken proposed regulations, making it harder for meaningful data protection laws to pass.
  • Difficulty in Employing Ethical Principles: Translating broad concepts like fairness and transparency into practical, enforceable legal and technical standards remains a significant challenge.

Understanding the Ethics Behind Data Governance

Examining data governance through different ethical lenses helps illuminate the multifaceted nature of the responsibilities and challenges involved. Each perspective offers unique insights into why current practices may be problematic and how they could be improved.

Deontological Perspective

From a deontological perspective, the focus is on the duty to respect user privacy and obtain informed, meaningful consent. Companies have a moral obligation to be transparent about how data is collected, used, and shared regardless of how profitable it may be to obscure these processes.

The deontological solution would be to require clear, standardized disclosures of data use and to support legislation that enforces transparency and user consent. This aligns with the moral duty to treat individuals with honesty and respect, regardless of the financial impact on companies. Users should be able to make informed decisions about their data without deception or manipulation.

Utilitarian Perspective

A utilitarian approach would weigh the overall consequences of the lack of data governance in the United States against those with stronger protections like Europe with the GDPR. It would aim to identify the outcome that maximizes benefit and minimizes harm to society as a whole.

The utilitarian solution is to implement national standards for data protection similar to GDPR, ensuring transparency, fairness, and accountability. Though it may require upfront costs and shifts in businesses, it maximizes benefit by reducing harm, increasing user trust, and ensuring a more ethical digital landscape.

Virtue Ethics Perspective

From a virtue ethics perspective, the dilemma centers on whether companies and policymakers are acting with integrity and in alignment with virtues that support a virtuous society. Obscuring data practices and manipulating legislation reflect a lack of moral character.

The virtue ethics solution involves the creation and maintenance of an ethical and responsible culture within companies and government. Organizations should strive for transparency, accountability, and respect for users, not because they are forced to, but because it is the most ethical path. Encouraging these virtues leads to a more trustworthy and prosperous digital society.
